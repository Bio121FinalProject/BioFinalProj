Water

Cannabis, being an extremely water-intensive crop, has the potential to deplete local bodies of water, thereby posing a threat to local wildlife and humans.
  • In the Northern California Coast, where a large portion of American marijuana is grown, an estimated 22 liters of water or more per plant per day are applied during the growing season
    • This equates to:
      • approximately 3 billion L per km2 of greenhouse-grown marijuana per growing season.
      • approximately 430 million L per km2 of outdoor-grown marijuana per growing season
    • Compared to other crops:
      • Wine: wine grapes on the California north coast are estimated to use a mean of 271 million L of water per km2 of vines per growing season
      • Higher than that of avocados, almonds, among other crops
  • This could deplete the water levels in some streams
    • A 2015 study evaluated water levels in 4 different streams and found that water demands for marijuana cultivation exceed streamflow during low-flow periods
    • Habitat loss has also been proven to occur
    • These problems can worsen in areas with severe droughts, especially in California where a large portion of U.S. marijuana is grown
